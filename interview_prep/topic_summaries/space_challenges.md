# Space Challenges — Topic Summary

## DSN Architecture

NASA's Deep Space Network provides the Earth-side infrastructure for interplanetary communication:

| Complex | Location | Longitude | Key Dishes |
|---------|----------|-----------|------------|
| Goldstone | California, USA | 243°E | 70m DSS-14, 34m DSS-24/25/26 |
| Madrid | Robledo, Spain | 355°E | 70m DSS-63, 34m DSS-54/55 |
| Canberra | Australia | 149°E | 70m DSS-43, 34m DSS-34/35/36 |

The three complexes are spaced 120° apart in longitude, ensuring at least one station has any deep-space target above the horizon at all times. Each complex has multiple 34-meter antennas that can be arrayed for higher gain.

AETHERIX integrates with DSN for the Earth ground tier (6 nodes): three traditional RF stations plus three optical ground stations co-located at or near existing DSN complexes. Optical ground stations require: high-altitude sites (low atmospheric turbulence), 1-meter+ receiving telescopes, adaptive optics, and superconducting nanowire single-photon detectors (SNSPDs).

## Lagrange Points: Why L4/L5

The Sun-Earth system has five Lagrange points where gravitational and centrifugal forces balance:

| Point | Distance from Earth | Stability | AETHERIX Use |
|-------|-------------------|-----------|--------------|
| L1 | 1.5M km sunward | Unstable (halo orbit) | Solar observatories (not used) |
| L2 | 1.5M km anti-sunward | Unstable (halo orbit) | JWST location (not used) |
| L3 | Opposite side of Sun | Unstable | Not used |
| **L4** | 60° ahead in orbit | **Stable** | **Relay + quantum repeater** |
| **L5** | 60° behind in orbit | **Stable** | **Relay + quantum repeater** |

AETHERIX selects ES-L4 and ES-L5 because:
1. **Stable equilibrium** — objects at L4/L5 remain there naturally (Trojan points). Station-keeping delta-v is near zero, saving fuel over the mission lifetime.
2. **Solar conjunction coverage** — At 60° offset, they maintain line-of-sight to both Earth and Mars even when the direct path passes behind the Sun. This provides 50–70% link availability during conjunction.
3. **Intermediate relay distance** — ~1 AU from Earth, shortening the deep-space hop into two more manageable segments.
4. **Quantum repeater co-location** — The relay satellite hosts both classical (optical/RF) and quantum (entanglement source) payloads.

The AETHERIX deep-space tier has 4 nodes: two at ES-L4 (primary relay + spare), two at ES-L5 (primary + spare).

## Hybrid Optical/RF Communication

| Parameter | Optical (1550 nm) | RF (Ka-band, 32 GHz) |
|-----------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Data rate (Earth-Mars) | 2–200 Mbps | 0.125–6 Mbps |
| Beam divergence | ~3.5 μrad | ~0.05° (0.87 mrad) |
| Pointing accuracy required | ~1 μrad | ~0.01° (175 μrad) |
| Weather sensitivity | High (clouds block) | Low (penetrates clouds) |
| Aperture size | 22 cm TX, 1 m RX | 3–5 m dish |
| Power | 5 W (36.99 dBm) | 20–100 W |
| TRL (Technology Readiness Level) | 7–8 (DSOC demo 2023) | 9 (flight proven) |

AETHERIX strategy: **optical primary, RF backup**.
- Optical handles routine high-throughput data (science images, bulk downloads).
- RF provides guaranteed connectivity when optical is unavailable (cloud cover, pointing loss, equipment fault).
- Both links can operate simultaneously for priority data (P0/P1 bundles on RF for reliability, bulk data on optical for throughput).
- Combined availability target: >95% versus ~75% for optical-only.

## Radiation Environment

The interplanetary radiation environment poses challenges for electronics:

| Environment | Source | Effect |
|-------------|--------|--------|
| Van Allen belts | Trapped protons/electrons | Single-event upsets (SEU), total ionising dose |
| Solar particle events | Solar flares, CMEs | Single-event latchup (SEL), burst errors |
| Galactic cosmic rays | Background | Long-term degradation |
| Mars orbit | No magnetic shield | Higher dose than LEO |

Mitigation strategies in AETHERIX:
- **Radiation-hardened processors**: LEON3/4 (ESA), RAD750 (BAE) — 10–100× more resistant to SEU than commercial parts.
- **Error-correcting memory**: EDAC (Error Detection And Correction) on all storage — SECDED (single-error correction, double-error detection) on every memory word.
- **Triple modular redundancy (TMR)**: Critical routing logic runs in triplicate with voting.
- **Watchdog timers**: Autonomous reset on latchup detection.
- **Shielding**: Tantalum/aluminium shielding for total dose management.

Impact on DTN: radiation-induced bit errors increase the link's bit error rate (BER). AETHERIX's LTP convergence layer handles this at the segment level — corrupted segments are retransmitted without requiring end-to-end retransmission. Store-and-forward buffers use checksums and scrubbing to prevent data corruption during multi-day storage.

## Power Constraints

Spacecraft power is finite and generated by solar panels (or RTGs for outer planets):

| Orbit | Solar irradiance | Panel efficiency | Typical power budget |
|-------|-----------------|------------------|----------------------|
| LEO (600 km) | 1,361 W/m² | 28–30% | 1–5 kW |
| GEO (35,786 km) | 1,361 W/m² | 28–30% | 5–15 kW |
| Mars orbit (1.5 AU) | ~590 W/m² | 28–30% | 1–3 kW |
| Deep space (1–2.5 AU) | 590–220 W/m² | Variable | 0.5–2 kW |

AETHERIX's 5 W laser transmitter seems modest, but the RF backup requires 20–100 W. The RL agent's energy penalty (ε = 0.01 per Wh) discourages energy-intensive routing — storing a bundle for 12 hours until a better contact window costs less energy than forwarding through three extra hops. At Mars, dust storms can reduce solar panel output by 30–50% for weeks, forcing the RL agent to prioritise power-saving routing decisions.

## Atmospheric Turbulence

Earth's atmosphere distorts optical beams through:

- **Scintillation**: Intensity fluctuations (twinkling) caused by turbulent cells. Reduces received signal by 3–10 dB.
- **Beam wander**: The beam centroid shifts due to large-scale turbulence.
- **Beam spreading**: Turbulence increases the effective beam divergence beyond diffraction limit.

Mitigation:
- **Adaptive optics (AO)**: Deformable mirror corrects wavefront distortion in real-time. Reduces scintillation loss to ~1–2 dB.
- **Site selection**: High-altitude, arid locations (e.g., Table Mountain, CA; Izaña, Tenerife; Siding Spring, Australia) have lower turbulence.
- **Spatial diversity**: AETHERIX's three optical ground stations provide geographic diversity — if one site is cloudy or turbulent, another may be clear.
- **Multi-beam**: transmitting multiple parallel beams reduces scintillation through averaging.

For Mars-to-Earth downlink, the 22 cm Mars transmitter produces a beam that spreads to ~500 km diameter at Earth distance. The 1 m ground telescope captures only the central ~0.000001% of this beam — hence the need for high-gain receivers and photon-counting detectors.

## Pointing Accuracy

Optical links require micro-radian pointing accuracy:

- Beam divergence at 1550 nm with 22 cm aperture: λ/D = 1.55×10⁻⁶/0.22 ≈ 7.0 μrad (half-angle).
- To keep 90% of power on the receiver, pointing must be within ~1 μrad.
- At 225M km, 1 μrad pointing error displaces the beam by 225 km.

Pointing is achieved through:
1. **Coarse pointing**: Star trackers + reaction wheels (accuracy ~0.01° = 175 μrad).
2. **Fine pointing**: Fast steering mirrors with beacon tracking (accuracy ~1 μrad).
3. ** beacon-aided acquisition**: Earth transmits a beacon laser that the spacecraft locks onto.

AETHERIX assumes 3 dB pointing loss in the link budget (conservative), implemented in `LinkBudgetCalculator` as `transmitter_pointing_loss_db`.

## Practice Questions

### Q1. "What happens if the optical link fails entirely? Does the system go down?"

No. AETHERIX's hybrid optical/RF design ensures RF backup is always available. The Ka-band RF link provides 125 kbps to 6 Mbps — sufficient for critical data (P0/P1 bundles, commands). During an optical failure, the system automatically falls back to RF. The RL agent adapts: with only RF available, it reduces forwarding rates, increases store durations, and prioritises high-priority bundles. If both optical and RF fail on a single link, store-and-forward holds bundles until the link recovers. At no point does a single link failure cause data loss — only delay.

### Q2. "Why place relays at L4/L5 instead of just using more ground stations?"

More ground stations don't solve the solar conjunction problem. During conjunction, the Sun is physically between Earth and Mars — no ground station on Earth has line-of-sight regardless of location. L4/L5 relays are 60° offset in Earth's orbit, giving them a line-of-sight to Mars that goes around the Sun. Additionally, L4/L5 are gravitationally stable (no station-keeping fuel needed), unlike L1/L2 which require continuous correction. Ground stations also can't extend QKD range — quantum repeaters must be in space. The trade-off: L4/L5 relays add 4 spacecraft (with launch costs), but they serve as both classical relays and quantum repeaters, and they provide conjunction coverage that no number of ground stations can match.

### Q3. "How do you handle radiation-induced errors in stored bundles?"

Three layers of protection. First, memory hardware uses EDAC (error-detecting-and-correcting) codes — SECDED on every 32-bit word corrects any single-bit flip and detects double-bit errors. Second, the DTN storage layer applies CRC32 checksums to each bundle; periodic scrubbing reads and verifies stored bundles, correcting soft errors before they accumulate. Third, LTP's retransmission mechanism handles errors that occur during transmission — if a segment arrives corrupted, the receiver sends a report segment requesting retransmission. The combination of hardware EDAC, software scrubbing, and protocol-level retransmission provides multiple independent error-detection paths.

### Q4. "What's the impact of a global dust storm on Mars operations?"

A global dust storm (occurring roughly every 3 Mars years, ~6 Earth years) affects AETHERIX in three ways: (1) Mars surface solar panels lose 30–80% of output, forcing power-constrained operation. The RL agent's energy penalty (ε = 0.01/Wh) becomes dominant, shifting policy toward store-and-wait rather than immediate transmission. (2) Atmospheric dust increases optical scattering, degrading surface-to-orbiter links by 5–15 dB. RF links are less affected. (3) Rovers and drones may need to hibernate, reducing data generation. The RL agent would observe declining buffer occupancy and link quality, learning to defer bulk data transfers until conditions improve. Pre-storm preparations (identified from orbital weather monitoring) include pre-charging batteries and uploading autonomous operation schedules.

### Q5. "Your link budget shows 10 dB margin. Is that enough?"

10 dB margin is conservative for deep-space optical links. It accounts for: atmospheric scintillation (1–3 dB after adaptive optics), pointing jitter (1–3 dB), component aging (1–2 dB), and unexpected losses. NASA's DSOC (Deep Space Optical Communications) demonstration in 2023 used similar margins successfully at Mars distances. However, 10 dB is the average-distance design point. At aphelion (401M km), the margin shrinks because FSPL increases by ~5 dB relative to average. At opposition (54.6M km), the margin is generous (~17 dB). If the margin goes negative at aphelion, AETHERIX reduces the data rate (from 200 Mbps to 2 Mbps) to maintain positive margin — a valid trade-off specified by the Shannon capacity equation: reducing bandwidth reduces the required SNR.

## Hybrid Optical/RF Link Analysis

AETHERIX's hybrid link controller manages simultaneous optical and RF transmission:

### Operating Modes

| Mode | Optical | RF | Trigger | Data Handling |
|------|---------|-----|---------|---------------|
| Dual-primary | Active | Active | Normal operations | P0/P1 on RF, P2+P3 on optical, P4 fills gaps |
| Optical-primary | Active | Standby | Clear weather | All priorities on optical, RF ready |
| RF-primary | Standby | Active | Cloud cover / optical fault | All traffic on RF at reduced rate |
| Emergency | Active | Active (boosted) | Critical event (P0 bundle) | P0 on both links simultaneously for redundancy |
| Conjunction-only | Inactive | Inactive | Solar conjunction (direct) | Store locally, use Lagrange relay path |

### Automatic Failover

The link controller monitors optical received signal level every 100 ms. If the signal drops below threshold for >5 seconds:

1. **Immediate**: All queued P0/P1 bundles are diverted to RF.
2. **Transient (<30 s)**: P2 bundles held in buffer; optical expected to recover.
3. **Sustained (>30 s)**: All traffic diverted to RF. Optical link enters recovery mode (beacon reacquisition).
4. **Recovery**: Optical beacon re-acquired → signal quality verified → optical resumes → traffic rebalanced.

Failover is transparent to the bundle layer — the convergence layer adapter switches the underlying transport without affecting custody state.

## Policy Engine with 5 Default Policies

AETHERIX's routing policy engine constrains the RL agent's decisions with operational rules. The engine evaluates policies before the RL agent selects an action:

### Policy Architecture

```
Bundle arrives → Policy Engine (rule check) → RL Agent (action selection) → CLA dispatch
```

If any policy rejects the RL agent's proposed action, the agent must select an alternative. Policies are evaluated in priority order; the first rejection is final.

### Default Policies

| Policy ID | Name | Rule | Rationale |
|-----------|------|------|-----------|
| POL-001 | **Critical Protection** | Never DROP bundles with priority P0 or P1. If buffer is full, expire P4 bundles first, then P3, then P2. | Emergency and high-science data must never be lost. |
| POL-002 | **Energy Budget** | If node's remaining energy < 20% of capacity, suppress FORWARDS on energy-intensive links (optical TX, high-power RF). Allow only STORE and low-power RF forwards. | Prevent battery depletion during dust storms or solar panel degradation. |
| POL-003 | **Loop Prevention** | If the proposed next-hop is in the bundle's hop-history (last 5 hops), reject and force re-selection. | Prevent routing loops caused by stale RL state or topology changes. |
| POL-004 | **Lifetime Gate** | If a bundle's remaining lifetime < estimated delivery time via the proposed path, downgrade priority and attempt shortest-delay path instead. | Prevent wasting resources on bundles that will expire before delivery. |
| POL-005 | **Conjunction Protocol** | During declared conjunction window, suppress all forwards to Earth-direct links. Force traffic through Lagrange relay path (ES-L4/ES-L5). | Avoid wasting transmission power on blocked direct path during conjunction. |

### Policy Evaluation

Each policy returns one of: `ALLOW`, `DENY`, or `DENY_WITH_SUGGESTION`. A `DENY_WITH_SUGGESTION` includes an alternative action (e.g., "DENY forward to node X; SUGGEST store for 2 hours until contact window opens"). The RL agent treats the suggestion as a strong bias but is not forced to follow it — this allows the agent to find potentially better alternatives.

### Custom Policies

The policy engine supports custom policies uploaded as DTN bundles (P1 priority, ML-DSA signed). This allows mission operators to inject new rules without software updates — for example: "POL-006: During Mars dust storm season, limit drone uplink to 1 Mbps and route all drone data through the nearest base hub." Custom policies are validated against a schema before activation.
