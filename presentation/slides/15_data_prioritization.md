## The problem
The Earth–Mars link is bandwidth-starved and only open during scheduled contacts.
When demand exceeds capacity, you must decide *what gets sent first* — and what waits.

## Four-tier data classification
| Tier | Class | Examples | BPv7 priority |
|------|-------|----------|---------------|
| **P0** | Emergency / Safety-critical | Health telemetry, collision avoidance, faults | EMERGENCY |
| **P1** | Mission-critical | Command ACKs, time-sensitive science events | HIGH_SCIENCE |
| **P2** | High-priority | Routine telemetry, scheduled science | STANDARD |
| **P4** | Low / Bulk | Housekeeping logs, file transfers, software images | BULK |

## Three levers
1. **Compression** (before transmission) — representative CCSDS-class ratios:
   - Telemetry → **CCSDS 121.0-B-3** (Rice) ≈ 3×
   - Imagery (lossy) → **CCSDS 122.0-B-2** (wavelet) ≈ 10×
   - Video → H.265 ≈ 50×
2. **Deadline-aware, preemptive QoS scheduling** — strict priority order, then earliest deadline; items that cannot meet their deadline are deferred to the next contact.
3. **BPv7 fragmentation** — a large low-priority bundle sends the portion that fits this contact and defers the remainder, so the link is never left idle.

## Headline numbers (AETHERIX model)
- Scenario: **30 Mbps, 15-minute contact, oversubscribed**.
- **5 / 6** items fully delivered in strict priority order; the 6 GB software update is **fragmented (41% this pass, remainder next)**.
- **100%** link utilization (no wasted bandwidth).
- **Emergency preemption**: a P0 alert preempts the in-progress transfer and goes out on a low-rate **direct-to-Earth** backup, then normal scheduling resumes.
